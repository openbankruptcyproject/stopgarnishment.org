IRS and State Tax Garnishment

Tax agencies have broader garnishment powers than regular creditors -- and different exemptions apply

IRS Tax Levies Are Different

The IRS does not need a court order to garnish your wages. Under the Internal Revenue Code, the IRS can issue a tax levy directly to your employer after sending you a Final Notice of Intent to Levy and a Notice of Your Right to a Hearing. This makes IRS garnishment faster and harder to challenge than regular creditor garnishment.

IRS levies also do not follow the standard 25% limit. Instead, the IRS uses a formula based on your filing status and number of dependents that determines the exempt amount. The rest -- often significantly more than 25% -- can be taken. For high-income earners, IRS levies can take 70% or more of each paycheck.

Stopping IRS Garnishment

You have several options for stopping an IRS levy: negotiate an installment agreement (monthly payment plan), request currently not collectible status if you cannot afford basic living expenses, submit an offer in compromise (settling for less than the full amount), or file for bankruptcy.

Filing bankruptcy stops IRS levies through the automatic stay, just like other garnishments. Chapter 13 can restructure tax debts over 3-5 years. Some older income taxes (more than 3 years old, filed more than 2 years ago, assessed more than 240 days ago) may even be dischargeable. For tax-specific information, see bankruptcytaxes.org.

State Tax Garnishment

State tax agencies also have enhanced collection powers, though they vary by state. Some states mirror the IRS process, while others require court orders. State tax liens and levies can affect your paycheck, bank accounts, and property.

State taxes follow different rules for discharge in bankruptcy than federal taxes. Check with a bankruptcy attorney to determine whether your state tax debt qualifies for discharge.

Student Loan Administrative Garnishment

Federal student loan servicers can garnish up to 15% of disposable earnings through "administrative wage garnishment" without a court order, after providing notice and an opportunity for a hearing. This is a lower percentage than standard garnishment but is in addition to any existing garnishments for other debts.

Private student loans must go through the regular court process for garnishment.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the IRS garnish my wages without a court order?

Yes. The IRS can issue a levy directly to your employer after sending required notices. They do not need a court judgment.

How much can the IRS take from my paycheck?

The IRS uses a formula based on filing status and dependents. The exempt amount can be as low as $200-300 per week, with the rest going to the IRS.

Can bankruptcy stop IRS garnishment?

Yes. Filing bankruptcy triggers the automatic stay, which stops IRS levies. Some older tax debts may even be dischargeable.

Can student loans be garnished without a court order?

Federal student loans can take up to 15% through administrative garnishment without a court order. Private student loans require a court judgment.
