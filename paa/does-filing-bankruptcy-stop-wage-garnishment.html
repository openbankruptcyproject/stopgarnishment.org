Does filing bankruptcy stop wage garnishment?

Yes. Filing bankruptcy immediately triggers the automatic stay, which stops all wage garnishments. The garnishment must stop within one pay period of the creditor and employer receiving notice. In some cases, garnished funds not yet distributed to the creditor can be recovered and returned to you.

The automatic stay is the most powerful tool for stopping garnishment, and it works immediately upon filing.

How It Works

  1. You file your bankruptcy petition with the court
  2. The automatic stay takes effect immediately under 11 U.S.C. Section 362(a)
  3. Notice is sent to your employer and the garnishing creditor
  4. The employer must stop withholding garnishment amounts
  5. The creditor must stop all collection activity

Recovering Garnished Funds

Funds garnished within 90 days before your bankruptcy filing may be recoverable as a "preference" under Section 547. If the garnished amount exceeds $600, the bankruptcy trustee can demand the money back from the creditor. In practice, this works best for larger amounts, as trustees may not pursue recovery for small preference claims.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 for Garnishment

Both chapters stop garnishment immediately. Chapter 7 eliminates the underlying debt entirely (for dischargeable debts) in 3-4 months, permanently ending any basis for garnishment. Chapter 13 replaces the garnishment with a structured repayment plan over 3-5 years at an amount based on your actual disposable income.

Non-Dischargeable Garnishments

Garnishment for child support and alimony is not stopped by the automatic stay in most circumstances. Tax garnishment may be temporarily stayed but the underlying tax debt may not be dischargeable. Student loan garnishment is stopped by the stay, but the debt requires an adversary proceeding to discharge.
