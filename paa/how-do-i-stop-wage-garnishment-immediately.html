How do I stop wage garnishment immediately?

The fastest way to stop wage garnishment is to file bankruptcy. The automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. Section 362 takes effect the moment your petition is filed and immediately stops all garnishments. Your employer must stop withholding within one pay period of receiving notice. Other options include negotiating with the creditor or filing a claim of exemption.

If your wages are being garnished, you need to know the fastest options for relief.

Option 1: File Bankruptcy (Fastest)

Filing a bankruptcy petition triggers the automatic stay immediately. Within hours of filing, you or your attorney can send notice to your employer and the garnishing creditor. Most employers stop the garnishment within one pay period. Any garnished funds not yet turned over to the creditor may be recoverable.

Option 2: Claim of Exemption

If your income is exempt from garnishment (Social Security, disability, VA benefits, or if garnishment leaves you below the federal minimum), you can file a claim of exemption with the court that issued the garnishment order. This requires a hearing and takes 1-3 weeks in most jurisdictions.

Option 3: Negotiate with the Creditor

Contact the creditor or their attorney and propose a voluntary payment plan. If the creditor agrees, they can file a motion to release the garnishment. This works best when you can offer a realistic payment plan that gives the creditor more than garnishment would.

Option 4: Vacate the Judgment

If you were never properly served with the lawsuit, or if there are other defenses, you may be able to vacate (undo) the underlying judgment. Without a valid judgment, the garnishment order falls. This is a longer process but may be appropriate in some cases.

How Much Can Be Garnished?

Federal law limits garnishment to 25% of disposable earnings or the amount by which your weekly income exceeds 30 times the federal minimum wage, whichever is less. Some states have lower limits. These limits do not apply to child support or tax garnishments, which can take more.
