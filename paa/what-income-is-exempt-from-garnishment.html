What income is exempt from garnishment?

Income exempt from garnishment includes Social Security benefits, SSI, VA disability and pension benefits, federal employee retirement, and Railroad Retirement benefits. Many states also protect workers' compensation, unemployment benefits, and public assistance. These exemptions apply automatically to direct deposits for 2 months after deposit.

Federal and state laws protect certain income sources from garnishment by private creditors. Knowing your exemptions is critical.

Federally Protected Income

  • Social Security: Retirement, disability (SSDI), and survivor benefits are exempt from garnishment by private creditors under 42 U.S.C. Section 407
  • Supplemental Security Income (SSI): Fully exempt
  • VA Benefits: Disability compensation, pension, and education benefits are exempt under 38 U.S.C. Section 5301
  • Federal Employee Retirement: Civil Service Retirement (CSRS) and Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS) benefits
  • Railroad Retirement: Benefits under the Railroad Retirement Act
  • Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) benefits

State-Protected Income

Most states also protect:

  • Workers' compensation benefits
  • Unemployment insurance benefits
  • Public assistance / welfare payments
  • State disability benefits
  • Certain pension and retirement benefits

The Bank Account Protection Rule

When exempt benefits are deposited into a bank account, the bank must automatically protect 2 months' worth of benefits from garnishment. This means if your Social Security direct deposit is $2,000/month, the first $4,000 in your account is protected without you having to do anything. This protection applies to federal benefit direct deposits.

Exceptions: What CAN Garnish Exempt Income

Federal benefits can be garnished for federal taxes (IRS), federal student loans, child support, and alimony. These exceptions are significant -- they mean that while private creditors cannot touch your Social Security, the government and family support obligations can.
