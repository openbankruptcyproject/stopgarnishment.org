Employment Protection Under Federal Law

Title III of the Consumer Credit Protection Act (15 U.S.C. 1674) prohibits employers from terminating an employee because of a single garnishment for any single indebtedness. This means your employer cannot fire you simply because one creditor has garnished your wages. Violation of this protection is a federal crime.

However, this protection has limits. Federal law only protects against termination for a single garnishment. If you have two or more garnishments from different creditors, the federal protection may not apply. Some states provide broader protection, prohibiting termination for multiple garnishments as well.

Garnishment and Job Applications

While federal law protects current employees from termination for a single garnishment, it does not address hiring decisions. Some employers run credit checks on job applicants and may consider garnishment history as a negative factor. If garnishment is affecting your ability to find employment, eliminating the underlying debt through bankruptcy can help.

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, employers must get your written permission before pulling your credit report and must provide you with a copy and a chance to dispute it before making an adverse decision based on the report.

What Your Employer Must Do

When your employer receives a garnishment order, they are legally required to comply. The employer must begin withholding the specified amount from your paycheck and remitting it to the garnishing creditor. Employers cannot charge you a fee for processing a garnishment in most states.

Your employer also has a duty to stop garnishment when they receive a bankruptcy filing notice, a satisfaction of judgment, or a court order releasing the garnishment. If your employer continues garnishing after receiving proper notice, they may be liable for the improperly withheld amounts.

Reducing the Impact

If garnishment is causing severe financial hardship, you may be able to file a motion with the court to reduce the garnishment amount. Courts can modify garnishment orders when the debtor demonstrates that the current amount causes undue hardship -- inability to pay rent, utilities, or other basic necessities.

Alternatively, filing for bankruptcy stops garnishment entirely and can eliminate the underlying debt. Learn about your options at howmuchdoesbankruptcycost.com.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my employer fire me for wage garnishment?

Federal law prohibits termination for a single garnishment. However, if you have multiple garnishments from different creditors, the federal protection may not apply. Some states offer broader protection.

Can an employer refuse to hire me because of garnishment?

Federal law does not specifically prohibit this. Employers can run credit checks with your permission and may consider garnishment history. Bankruptcy can help by eliminating the underlying debts.

Can my employer charge me for processing garnishment?

Most states prohibit employers from charging employees for garnishment processing costs.

How do I ask the court to reduce my garnishment?

File a motion for reduction or modification with the court that issued the garnishment order, demonstrating financial hardship.
