Traditional wage garnishment does not work for self-employed people -- but creditors have other tools

Why Traditional Garnishment Does Not Apply

Wage garnishment only works when you have an employer who can withhold money from your paycheck. If you are self-employed, a freelancer, or a gig worker, there is no employer to serve with a garnishment order. This does not mean you are immune from collection -- creditors simply use different tools.

Instead of wage garnishment, creditors pursuing self-employed individuals typically use bank account levies, property liens, and asset seizure. These methods can be even more disruptive than wage garnishment because they can freeze your entire bank account rather than taking a percentage of each paycheck.

Bank Levies and Business Accounts

The most common collection tool against self-employed individuals is the bank levy. A creditor with a judgment can obtain a court order directing your bank to freeze and turn over the funds in your account. If your business and personal funds are in the same account, the entire balance may be at risk.

Separating business and personal accounts provides some protection, as the creditor may only have a judgment against you personally. However, if you are a sole proprietor, there is no legal separation between you and your business. For LLCs and corporations, the distinction is stronger, but creditors may still try to reach business assets. Learn more at bankaccountlevy.com.

1099 Garnishment and Account Receivable Seizure

Some creditors attempt to garnish payments owed to you by your clients. This is sometimes called "account receivable garnishment" or "non-earnings garnishment." The creditor serves a garnishment order on your client, directing them to pay the creditor instead of you.

This is a particularly aggressive tactic that can damage your business relationships. If a client receives a garnishment order related to your personal debt, they may decide to stop working with you entirely.

Bankruptcy Protection for the Self-Employed

Bankruptcy is often the most effective solution for self-employed individuals facing aggressive collection. The automatic stay stops all collection activity -- bank levies, property liens, and account receivable garnishment. Chapter 13 can restructure debts while you continue operating your business.

Self-employed individuals may also qualify for Chapter 7 if their income after business expenses falls below the state median. The means test determines eligibility.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can self-employed people have wages garnished?

Traditional wage garnishment does not apply because there is no employer. However, creditors can use bank levies, property liens, and account receivable garnishment instead.

Can creditors garnish payments from my clients?

Yes. Creditors can serve garnishment orders on your clients, directing them to pay the creditor instead of you. This is called account receivable garnishment.

How do I protect my business bank account?

Keep business and personal funds separate. If you operate as an LLC or corporation, business assets may have additional protection. Consult an attorney about asset protection strategies.

Can I file bankruptcy and keep my business?

Yes. Many self-employed individuals file bankruptcy and continue operating. Chapter 13 is often the best option for business owners who need to restructure debt while maintaining operations.
