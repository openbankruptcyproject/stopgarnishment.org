What Is a Bank Levy?

A bank levy (also called a bank garnishment or attachment) is when a creditor obtains a court order directing your bank to freeze funds in your account and turn them over to the creditor. Unlike wage garnishment, which takes a percentage of each paycheck over time, a bank levy can seize everything in your account up to the judgment amount in a single action.

Bank levies are particularly devastating because they happen without warning. You may discover the levy only when your debit card is declined or checks bounce. The bank freezes your account immediately upon receiving the levy order, then typically holds the funds for a waiting period (often 21 days) before releasing them to the creditor.

Protecting Your Bank Account

Federal benefits (Social Security, SSI, VA) deposited by direct deposit are automatically protected for two months under the 2013 federal banking regulation. Banks must review your account for protected federal deposits and exclude them from the levy automatically.

For other funds, you must file an exemption claim with the court. State exemptions vary widely -- some states protect a specific dollar amount, others protect certain types of funds. Act quickly, as the waiting period before funds are released to the creditor is limited. For a complete guide, visit bankaccountlevy.com.

Bank Levy vs. Wage Garnishment Compared

Wage garnishment takes a portion of each paycheck on an ongoing basis. A bank levy seizes a lump sum from your account. Wage garnishment is limited to 25% of disposable earnings, while a bank levy can take the entire account balance (minus exempt funds). Both require a court judgment first (for most debts), and both can be stopped by filing bankruptcy.

Creditors often use both tools simultaneously -- garnishing wages and levying accounts. If you are facing both, bankruptcy provides the broadest protection by stopping all collection activity through the automatic stay.

How Bankruptcy Stops Bank Levies

Filing bankruptcy immediately stops pending bank levies through the automatic stay. If the bank has already frozen your funds but has not yet released them to the creditor, you may be able to recover those funds. If the levy was executed within 90 days before filing, the funds may be recoverable as a preference payment under 11 U.S.C. 547.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a bank levy?

A court order directing your bank to freeze and turn over funds in your account to a creditor. Unlike wage garnishment, it can seize your entire account balance in one action.

Can they take my Social Security from my bank account?

No. Federal benefits deposited by direct deposit are automatically protected for two months under federal banking regulations.

How do I stop a bank levy?

File an exemption claim with the court immediately. If you have multiple debts, filing bankruptcy stops all levies through the automatic stay.

Can I open a new bank account to avoid a levy?

Opening a new account does not eliminate the debt. The creditor can obtain new levy orders against any account they discover. Bankruptcy is the only way to permanently stop levies by eliminating the underlying debt.
