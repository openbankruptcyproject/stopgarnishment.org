How Wage Garnishment Works

From lawsuit to paycheck deduction -- understand every step of the garnishment process so you can fight back

← Home

Step 1: The Creditor Gets a Judgment

Before a regular creditor can garnish your wages, they must first sue you and win. The creditor files a lawsuit, you are served with a summons and complaint, and either you lose at trial or a default judgment is entered because you did not respond. This judgment is the legal authorization that allows the creditor to use collection tools like garnishment.

Important exception: the IRS, state tax agencies, and federal student loan servicers can garnish without a court judgment. Child support garnishment is authorized by the family court order itself. For regular debts (credit cards, medical bills, personal loans), the creditor must go through the court first.

Step 2: The Garnishment Order

After obtaining a judgment, the creditor files a separate motion or application for a garnishment order (sometimes called a "writ of garnishment" or "earnings withholding order"). The court issues the order, which is then served on your employer.

You should receive notice of the garnishment order and an opportunity to claim exemptions. The notice period varies by state -- some give you as little as 5 days, others up to 30 days. This is your window to claim any exemptions that apply to your income.

Step 3: Employer Withholding

Your employer is legally required to comply with the garnishment order. They must calculate the correct withholding amount (the lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or the amount above 30 times minimum wage), deduct it from each paycheck, and send it to the court or creditor.

The employer continues withholding until the judgment is fully paid, the garnishment is released by court order, or you file for bankruptcy. Some garnishments last for months or years depending on the size of the debt.

How to Fight Back

Your options include claiming exemptions (protected income types), challenging the underlying judgment (if it was entered by default), negotiating with the creditor for a payment plan or settlement, filing a hardship motion to reduce the garnishment amount, or filing for bankruptcy.

The most effective defense depends on your specific situation. If the garnishment is for a dischargeable debt and you qualify, bankruptcy provides the most comprehensive relief by stopping the garnishment and eliminating the debt. Check your eligibility at 1328f.com.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do creditors need a court order to garnish wages?

Most creditors must first sue you, win a judgment, then obtain a garnishment order. Exceptions: IRS, state tax agencies, federal student loans, and child support.

How long does garnishment last?

Until the judgment is fully paid, the court releases the garnishment, or you file for bankruptcy. This can be months or years depending on the debt amount.

Can I settle with the creditor to stop garnishment?

Yes. Many creditors will agree to a payment plan or lump-sum settlement to stop garnishment. Get any agreement in writing before making payments.

What if I was not properly served in the original lawsuit?

If you were not properly served, the judgment may be voidable. File a motion to vacate the default judgment. See defaultjudgment.org for guidance.
