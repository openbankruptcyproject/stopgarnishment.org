Federal and State Exemptions That Shield Your Money

Federally Protected Income

Federal law categorically protects several income types from garnishment by private creditors. Social Security benefits, Supplemental Security Income (SSI), Veterans Administration benefits, federal civilian and military retirement pay, Railroad Retirement benefits, and federal student financial aid cannot be taken by credit card companies, medical bill collectors, or other private creditors.

These protections exist because Congress determined these benefits are essential to the survival of recipients who are typically elderly, disabled, or veterans. The protection follows the money -- when these benefits are deposited into a bank account, they remain exempt for at least two months under federal banking regulations.

Exceptions to Federal Protection

While private creditors cannot touch these benefits, the federal government has broader powers. Social Security can be garnished for federal tax debts (up to 15%), child support and alimony, non-tax debts owed to other federal agencies, and certain crime victim restitution orders. SSI, however, is completely exempt from all garnishment, including by the government.

State tax agencies also have varying powers to garnish federal benefits depending on the state. Check with your state's consumer protection office for specifics.

State Exemptions

Many states provide additional protections beyond federal law. Some states exempt workers' compensation benefits, unemployment insurance, state retirement benefits, and disability insurance from garnishment. Several states protect "head of household" earners, sometimes shielding up to 100% of wages from garnishment for consumer debts.

State exemption laws vary dramatically. A head of household exemption that protects all wages in Florida may not exist in your state. Check your specific state exemptions at bankruptcyexemptionsbystate.com.

Claiming Your Exemption

With the exception of the automatic protection for federal benefits in bank accounts, you must actively claim most exemptions. When you receive a garnishment notice, you typically have a limited window (10-30 days depending on your state) to file an exemption claim with the court that issued the garnishment order.

To claim the exemption, you file a form (sometimes called a "Claim of Exemption" or "Exemption Affidavit") with the court, identifying which income or funds are exempt and providing documentation. If you miss the deadline, you may lose the right to claim the exemption for that specific garnishment action.

When Exemptions Are Not Enough

If your income is partially exempt but you are still losing too much to garnishment, or if you are facing garnishment on multiple debts, bankruptcy provides the most comprehensive relief. The automatic stay immediately stops all garnishment, and the discharge permanently eliminates the underlying debts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Social Security be garnished?

Not by private creditors. The federal government can garnish Social Security for taxes, child support, and certain government debts. SSI is completely exempt from all garnishment.

What income is completely exempt from garnishment?

Social Security, SSI, VA benefits, federal retirement, Railroad Retirement, and federal student aid are all protected from private creditor garnishment.

Does exempt income stay protected in my bank account?

Federal benefits deposited by direct deposit are automatically protected for two months. After that, they may lose protection as they commingle with other funds.

Do I need to claim my exemption?

For bank account levies on federal benefits, the bank must automatically protect two months of direct deposits. For other situations, you must file an exemption claim with the court within the deadline on your garnishment notice.
